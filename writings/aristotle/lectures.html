Lecture Course: Aristotle on Living Well

by Peter Saint-Andre

Last Updated: 2019-07-13

An experimental outline for a lecture course about Aristotle's views on the good life.

LECTURE 1 - eudaimonia

  • About me
  • About you
  • About Aristotle
  • Why study Aristotle's writings on ethics? Not to learn what Aristotle wrote, but to live better
  • Who can benefit? Those who have life experience and are basically good
  • But there are difficulties: the ancient Greek language, Aristotle's dense style, the extremely foreign culture of ancient Greece, the pre-Christian presuppositions of Aristotle's philosophy
  • Furthermore, our source materials are not consistent - there are subtle and not-so-subtle differences among Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics, Eudemian Ethics, and Politics
  • Yet all of these books are focused on one core concept: eudaimonia
  • Although the term is often translated as "happiness", it is much more than just a feeling
  • Eudaimonia is practical, rational activity grounded in excellence of character and decision-making
  • Another way to look at it: eudaimonia is an enjoyable combination of eupraxia (successful action) and euboulia (successful reflection)
  • Thus eudaimonia is a matter of actualizing your potential: it is an achievement, a completion or perfection of human nature - like hitting the bull's eye in archery
  • It's true that this experience is enjoyable, but the enjoyment is grounded in human nature and the circumstances and singularity of your own life
  • However, there are several ways to go astray or miss the target: total corruption of character (pursuing bad and wrong goals in life), impulsiveness (knowing what's right and good but not doing it), even mere self-control (doing what's right and good but doing so reluctantly or painfully)
  • What are the causes or success or failure in both action and reflection? Nature, nurture, and choice (this opens up big questions of personal responsibility!)

LECTURE 2 - relationships

  • Human beings as social animals
  • Philia (usually translated as "friendship") covers a wide range of personal relationships, from parental love to chosen friendships to business interactions to civic benevolence
  • Aristotle uncovers three different foundations for personal relationships: enjoyment vs. personal gain vs. excellence of character
  • Marriage: ancient and modern (plus: the status of women in ancient society)
  • Philia vs. eros
  • Close, chosen friendships
  • Child rearing, family, parental love
  • Community (civic friendship)
  • Love of self in Aristotle - are you your own best friend?
  • A friend as another self
  • Relationships as a source of self-knowledge
  • Modern individualism vs. ancient ways of life
  • Bridging the gap between individualism and altruism

LECTURE 3 - character

  • For Aristotle, ethics is the art of character (ethos), not a code of moral rules as in Christianity or modern ethics
  • For Aristotle, arete is not "virtue" but excellence or thriving
  • The four aretai in ancient Greek thought: courage, moderation, justice, wisdom (and often piety)
  • Aristotleâ€™s expanded list: generosity, benevolence, etc.
  • Not just acting appropriately but also reacting appropriately; excellence of character involves feeling and emotion
  • And there are differing motivations for good action (avoidance of punishment, shame, decency, love of honor, doing what is beautifully right): the example of courage
  • The highest intention is taking action for the sake of what is beautifully right (kalos)
  • Doing what is beautifully right and fitting for the occasion and in a particular context
  • Going astray is not right or fitting because it veers mindlessly toward one extreme or another
  • Excellence of character is a mindful balance between these extremes
  • Yet these extremes are a matter of missing the mark - not of sinning (a post-Aristotelian, Christian concept)
  • Thus there is always a spectrum of behavior: not black and white, not good and evil, but more or less on target
  • Going astray is usually a matter of being lured by pleasure or of avoiding pain
  • Aristotle's list of aretai reflects the deeply social nature of personal interactions in ancient Greek communities
  • But modern society is very different
  • Modern excellences includes excellences like productiveness and frugality, wider notions of courage, etc.
  • What is involved in becoming good? Education, training, upbringing
  • Mindful practice, not mindless habit
  • Excellence as an integration of character and thought

LECTURE 4 - thought and choice

  • Human beings as rational animals: one is best led by the part of one's person that "has reason", with the part that "listens to reason" following
  • Logos as reason-and-speech (but this too is social: learning to speak correctly and learning to act and react correctly both happen in a human context of family and community)
  • Practical wisdom as excellence of thinking and choosing
  • Orthos logos as "straight thinking"
  • Orthos orexis as "straight reaching"
  • Choice as desiderative desire or charged thinking
  • Here again, pleasure and pain can lead you astray
  • Balance revisited: choice as a way to guide or counteract your impulses
  • These matters are contextual; choice involves insight into particulars
  • There are no hard-and-fast rules for excellent choice (euboulia) and excellent conduct (eupraxia)
  • Yet persuasion and counsel play a major role, especially from family, friends, and community
  • In maturity, you engage in reflection, i.e., taking counsel with yourself
  • Applying Aristotelian dialectic to large decisions (philosophical practice)...
  • 1. reaching an impasse
  • 2. asking the right questions
  • 3. understanding the concrete situation
  • 4. analyzing your options
  • 5. synthesizing your insights and values
  • 6. making a choice / coming up with a plan
  • Once you have made choices and plans, action is not mindless but it does not require such deep reflection

LECTURE 5 - crafting a life

  • What makes a suitable goal (telos) for life?
  • Preconditions for eudaimonia (e.g., health) vs. eudaimonia itself
  • Wealth, fame, power (etc.) as inherently unbounded, unnatural, and unstable
  • Excellence of character and thought are natural activities
  • Unleisured activity exists for the sake of leisured activity
  • Work, play, and serious leisure
  • What free people do: learning, culture, friendship, etc.
  • Enjoyment of free activity
  • The philanthropic life
  • The love of wisdom
  • Divine activity as a model for human activity?
  • Sagacity and the life of the mind
  • The differing perspectives of the Nicomachean Ethics and Eudemian Ethics
  • What is Aristotle's settled view?
  • The life of complete excellence: kalokagathia in the Eudemian Ethics as "the right and the good"
  • Hitting the target is not just a matter of succeeding temporarily or in one area of life, but of crafting a life worth living
  • Aristotle's "three lives" of pleasure, community involvement, and intellectual inquiry: a thought experiment about how you'd structure your life if you didn't have to work
  • In modern times, there are other lives we consider worth living, such as professional achievement and artistic creativity - how do they fit into an Aristotelian perspective and why did Aristotle not consider them?
  • Do the "three lives" make sense in the modern world?
  • Does your life need one central task or focus?
  • Maintaining multiple areas of focus
  • A "portfolio management" approach to thriving as a human being
  • Crafting a life
