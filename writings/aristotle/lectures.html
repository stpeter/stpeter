Lecture Course: Aristotle on Living Well

by Peter Saint-Andre

Last Updated: 2019-07-10

Note: This is an experimental outline for a lecture course about Aristotle's views on the good life.

LECTURE 1 - eudaimonia

  • About me
  • About you
  • About Aristotle
  • Why study Aristotle's writings on ethics? To live better
  • Who can benefit? Those who have life experience and are basically good
  • But there are difficulties: language, style, culture, philosophy
  • And our source materials are not consistent: NE, EE, MM, Politics
  • All of these books are focused on one core concept: eudaimonia
  • The term is often translated as "happiness" but it is more than just a feeling
  • Eudaimonia is practical, rational activity grounded in excellence of character and decision-making
  • Another way to look at it: eudaimonia is an enjoyable combination of eupraxia (successful action) and euboulia (successful reflection)
  • Thus eudaimonia is a matter of actualizing your potential: it is an achievement and a completion or perfection of human nature, like hitting the bull's eye in archery
  • Of course this is enjoyable, but it's an enjoyment grounded in human nature and your own context
  • However, there are several ways to go astray or miss the target: total corruption of character (pursuing bad and wrong goals in life), impulsiveness (knowing what's right and good but not doing it), even mere self-control (doing what's right and good but reluctantly or painfully)
  • What are the causes or success or failure? Nature, nurture, and choice (this opens up big questions of personal responsibility!)
  • Hitting the target consists not just in succeeding temporarily or in one area of life, but in crafting a life worth living
  • The "three lives" of pleasure, community involvement, and intellectual inquiry: consider them a thought experiment of how you'd structure your life if you didn't have to work
  • In modern times there are other lives we consider worth living, such as professional achievement and artistic creativity - how do they fit into an Aristotelian perspective and why did Aristotle not consider them?

LECTURE 2 - relationships

  • Human beings as social animals
  • Philia (usually translated as "friendship") covers a wide range of personal relationships, from parental love to chosen friendships to business interactions to civic benevolence
  • Different foundations for personal relationships: enjoyment vs. personal gain vs. excellence of character
  • Marriage: ancient and modern (plus: the status of women in ancient society)
  • Love and eros
  • Close, chosen friendships
  • Child rearing, family, parental love
  • Community (civic friendship)
  • Love of self in Aristotle
  • A friend as another self
  • Modern individualism
  • Bridging the gap between individualism and altruism
  • Relationships as a source of self-knowledge

LECTURE 3 - character

  • The four virtues in ancient Greek thought: courage, moderation, justice, wisdom (and often piety)
  • Aristotleâ€™s expanded list: generosity, benevolence, etc.
  • Differing motivations for good action: the example of courage
  • Not just acting appropriately but also reacting appropriately; excellence of character involves feeling and emotion
  • The highest intention: taking action for the sake of what is beautifully right
  • Excellence of character as a balance between extremes
  • Yet these extremes are a matter of missing the mark - not of sinning
  • There is always a spectrum of behavior: not black and white, not good and evil
  • Pleasure and pain as factors that lead you astray
  • The deeply social nature of Greek virtues
  • But society is different now
  • Modern excellences: productiveness, frugality, etc.
  • What is involved in becoming good? Education, training, upbringing
  • Mindful practice, not mindless habit
  • Excellence as an integration of character and thought

LECTURE 4 - thought and choice

  • Human beings as rational animals
  • Logos as reason-and-speech (although this too is social: learning to speak correctly and learning to act and react correctly both happen in a human context of family and community)
  • Practical wisdom as excellence of thinking and choosing
  • Orthos logos as "straight thinking"
  • Orthos orexis as "straight reaching"
  • Choice as desiderative desire or charged thinking
  • Here again, pleasure and pain can lead you astray
  • Balance revisited: choice as a way to guide or counteract your impulses
  • These matters are contextual and choice involves insight into particulars
  • Unfortunately, there are no solid rules for choice and conduct
  • Yet persuasion and counsel play a major role, especially from family, friends, and community
  • Reflection as taking counsel with yourself
  • Applying Aristotelian dialectic to philosophical practice...
  • 1. reaching an impasse
  • 2. asking the right questions
  • 3. understanding the concrete situation
  • 4. analyzing your options
  • 5. synthesizing your insights and values
  • 6. making a choice / coming up with a plan

LECTURE 5 - crafting a life

  • What makes a suitable goal (telos) for life?
  • Preconditions for happiness (e.g., health)
  • Wealth, fame, power (etc.) as inherently unbounded, unnatural, and unstable
  • Excellence of character and thought are natural activities
  • Unleisured activity exists for the sake of leisured activity
  • Work, play, and serious leisure
  • What free people do
  • Enjoyment of free activity
  • The philanthropic life
  • The love of wisdom
  • Divine activity as a model for human activity?
  • Sagacity and the life of the mind
  • The differing perspectives of the Nicomachean Ethics and Eudemian Ethics
  • What is Aristotle's settled view?
  • The life of complete excellence: kalokagathia as "the right and the good"
  • Do the "three lives" make sense in the modern world?
  • Does your life need one central task or focus?
  • Maintaining multiple areas of focus
  • A "portfolio management" approach to thriving as a human being
  • Crafting a life
