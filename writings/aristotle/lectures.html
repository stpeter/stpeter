Lecture Course: Aristotle on Living Well

by Peter Saint-Andre

Last Updated: 2019-07-06

Note: This is an experimental outline for a lecture course about Aristotle's views on the good life.

LECTURE 1 - eudaimonia

  • About me
  • About you
  • About Aristotle
  • Why study Aristotle? To live better
  • Who can benefit? Those who have life experience and are basically good
  • But there are difficulties: language, style, culture, philosophy
  • And our source materials are not consistent: NE, EE, MM, Politics
  • All of these books are focused on one core concept: eudaimonia
  • Eudaimonia is attended by enjoyment but is not merely a feeling of happiness; it is practical activity grounded in excellence of character and decision-making
  • Another way to look at it: eudaimonia is an enjoyable combination of eupraxia (successful action) and euboulia (successful reflection)
  • Thus eudaimonia is an achievement and a completion or perfection of human nature, like hitting the bull's eye in archery
  • But there are several ways to go astray or miss the target: total corruption of character (pursuing bad and wrong goals in life), impulsiveness (knowing what's right and good but not doing it), even mere self-control (doing what's right and good but reluctantly or painfully)
  • What are the causes or success or failure? Nature, nurture, and choice
  • Hitting the target consists not just in succeeding temporarily or in one area of life, but in crafting a life worth living
  • The "three lives" of pleasure, community involvement, and intellectual inquiry: consider them a thought experiment of how you'd structure your life if you didn't have to work
  • In modern times there are other lives we consider worth living, such as professional achievement and artistic creativity - how do they fit into an Aristotelian perspective?

  • The basis of relationships: enjoyment vs. advantage vs. thriving
  • Marriage: ancient and modern
  • Child rearing
  • Community (civic friendship)
  • Love of self in Aristotle
  • A friend as another self
  • Modern individualism
  • Bridging the gap between individualism and altruism
  • Relationships as a source of self-knowledge

LECTURE 3 - character

  • The four virtues in ancient Greek thought: courage, moderation, justice, wisdom (and often piety)
  • Aristotleâ€™s expanded list: generosity, benevolence, etc.
  • Motivations for good action: the example of courage
  • Acting for what is beautifully right
  • Excellence of character as a balance between extremes
  • Yet these extremes are a matter of missing the mark - not of sinning
  • Always a spectrum of behavior: not black and white, not good and evil
  • Pleasure and pain
  • The deeply social nature of Greek virtues
  • But society is different now
  • Modern excellences: productiveness, frugality, etc.
  • What is involved in becoming good?
  • Mindful practice, not mindless habit
  • Excellence is an integration of character and thought

LECTURE 4 - thought and choice

  • Human beings as rational animals
  • Logos as reason-and-speech (this too is social)
  • Persuasion and counsel
  • Taking counsel with yourself
  • Orthos logos as straight thinking
  • Orthos orexis as straight reaching
  • Desiderative desire, charged thinking
  • Pleasure and pain can lead you astray
  • Balance revisited: counteracting impulses
  • Choice involves insight into particulars
  • There are no solid rules for choice and conduct
  • Practices of thought and persuasion
  • Planning and forethought
  • Practical wisdom
  • Sagacity and the lure of the life of the mind

LECTURE 5 - crafting a life

  • What makes a suitable goal (telos) for life?
  • Wealth, fame, power (etc.) as inherently unbounded, unnatural, and unstable
  • Excellence of character and thought are natural activities
  • Divine activity as a model for human activity
  • Unleisured activity exists for the sake of leisured activity
  • Work, play, and serious leisure
  • What free people do
  • Enjoyment of free activity
  • Philanthropy
  • The love of wisdom
  • Kalokagathia - the right and the good
  • Action for the sake of what is beautifully right
  • Does your life need one central task or focus?
  • The role of projects and relationships
  • Thinking in terms of a life
  • Philosophical practice: impasse, question, understanding, analysis, synthesis, plan
